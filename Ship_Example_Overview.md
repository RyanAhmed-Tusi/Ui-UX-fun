# Creative Brief: Immersive Website — Ship Supply Company

## Project Title Concept
*"From Cabin to Cosmos"* — A scroll-driven cinematic journey that begins inside a vessel and expands to a global view of the company's reach.

---

## Experience Overview

This is a single-page, scroll-driven cinematic experience. There is no traditional navigation between sections — the entire site is one continuous visual journey told through camera movement, environmental transitions, and layered storytelling. The background is never static. It evolves with every scroll — from a closed cabin window to the open ocean, beneath the waves, back to the sea surface, up into the sky, and ultimately out to space. The user is always moving through a world, not reading a page.

---

## Section 1 — The Preloader

The site opens in darkness. Gradually, the interior of a ship's cabin materialises — warm, low ambient light, riveted steel walls, the texture of a vessel at sea. Centred on screen is a **porthole window**, firmly shut, its circular metal frame and bolted hatch giving it weight and realism.

A subtle ambient sound of distant waves and the creaking of a hull plays softly (optional, muted by default with a toggle).

A minimal loading indicator sits just below or above the porthole — clean typography, no progress bars. Something understated: *"Setting sail…"* or simply the company name revealing letter by letter.

The background itself breathes — a very subtle parallax drift, as if the cabin is gently rocking. This micro-motion signals immediately: this is not a static website.

**Once loading completes:** The porthole's locking mechanism rotates and releases in a smooth mechanical animation. The hatch swings open — or more precisely, the circular viewport iris-wipes open, the bolts unscrew in sequence, and the window frame retracts. Light pours in from outside. The ocean is revealed through the opening.

The transition from preloader to hero is seamless — no cut, no fade to black. The world simply opens.

---

## Section 2 — The Hero

The porthole is now fully open. Through it: a vast, living ocean scene. On the horizon, a ship sits in the water — distant but present, slightly silhouetted against the sky.

This background scene is **alive**. It is not a video loop or a static image. The ocean surface moves with gentle wave displacement. Clouds drift slowly across the sky. The distant ship sways imperceptibly — a subtle bob on the water. Light shimmers off the ocean surface, and a faint light source — a lighthouse on the horizon, or the ship's own navigation lights — pulses with a soft, slow rhythm. The entire scene has the quality of watching through a window on a calm day at sea.

**Layout:**
- The porthole window sits centred, framing the ocean view like a lens
- The company logo is placed within the porthole frame — overlaid on the view, as if etched onto the glass
- Hero headline and supporting text are positioned on either side of the porthole, flanking it — left column and right column, giving a symmetrical, editorial feel
- A minimal scroll indicator at the bottom — perhaps a downward chevron or an animated line

**Visual tone:** Cinematic, expansive, authoritative. The company feels as vast as the ocean behind it.

---

## Section 3 — The Scroll Transition (Hero → Ocean)

*This is not a section — it is a continuous transition that unfolds across the first phase of scrolling. It lasts approximately 200–300 viewport heights of scroll distance, all while the user is pinned in place and the world moves around them.*

**As scroll begins:**

The hero text on either side of the porthole begins to drift outward — left text sliding left, right text sliding right — and simultaneously fades out. They disappear like notes carried away by wind.

The navbar does not exist yet. As scroll progresses to a threshold point, the navbar slides in from above — smooth, unhurried, already composed — and locks to the top of the viewport. Simultaneously, the logo, which had been centred on the porthole, animates upward and scales down, sliding into its place in the navbar's top-left corner. The navbar is now anchored.

**The camera begins to move:**

The porthole frame, rather than the camera moving toward it, appears to *expand*. The round window grows. The ocean view fills more and more of the screen. The cabin walls narrow in from the sides. The user is approaching the window.

Then the porthole frame disappears entirely — the ocean fills the full viewport. The user has passed through the glass. They are outside, hovering above the water.

**The descent:**

The horizon line rises. The camera tilts and descends. The ocean surface approaches. The scene darkens at the edges. And then — the surface breaks. The camera passes through the water.

The world submerges. The palette shifts: rich deep teal, shafts of light filtering from above, distant underwater silence rendered visually through a blue depth gradient. The ocean is now a texture, a field, a background — no longer a view.

---

## Section 4 — About (Underwater)

The camera has settled underwater. The background is a serene, softly animated ocean floor — not dark and threatening, but calm and deep. A slow, ambient drift of particles and light refractions keeps it alive.

The about section text appears centred on screen. But it does not arrive all at once.

**The scroll-reveal text effect:** The text begins nearly invisible — words rendered at very low opacity, existing but unreadable, like text seen through frosted glass. As the user scrolls through this section, words gradually resolve into full opacity — not all at once, but sequentially, phrase by phrase, sometimes word by word. The effect is that the content *crystallises* as you move through it, as if the depth is slowly giving up what it holds. By the time the user has scrolled to the end of the about section, every word is fully visible and fully legible.

This is the company's story: who they are, how long they have been sailing, what they stand for.

---

## Section 5 — Company Facts (Surfacing, Sea Level)

**The ascent begins.** The underwater palette brightens. The light from above intensifies. The ocean surface approaches from above — and breaks. The camera rises through, and the world is sky and sea again.

The background settles at sea level — a wide, open ocean view. A ship appears on the horizon and, as the section fully loads in, slowly drifts into a more prominent position in the frame. The background returns to its living quality: gentle wave animation, cloud drift, the ship moving with the water.

**The facts tiles enter:** A set of cards — four to six in a horizontal or grid arrangement — descend from the top of the viewport, settling into position like objects lowering onto a surface. Each card carries a key company statistic: years of operation, ports served, vessels supplied, countries covered.

**Hover interaction:** Each card has a 3D flip effect on hover. The front face shows the number and label. The card flips — a full 180° rotation on the Y-axis with correct perspective — revealing the back face, which holds a brief context sentence that gives the number meaning. The flip is smooth, physics-aware, with a slight ease-in and ease-out.

---

## Section 6 — Supply Categories (Into the Blueprint)

**As scroll continues,** the camera begins to move toward the ship on the horizon. The ship grows larger — slowly at first, then with increasing intention. The facts cards slide downward and out of frame as the camera advances.

The ship fills the viewport. And then something unexpected happens: the photorealistic ship begins to *dissolve* into a technical illustration — a naval blueprint. Clean white line art on a deep navy blue background. Grid lines. Dimension markers. Labelled compartments. The transformation is gradual, driven by scroll — the real ship and the blueprint co-exist for a moment, then the blueprint fully takes over.

**Inside the blueprint:** The camera continues forward — the user passes through the outer hull of the blueprint illustration and navigates inside the ship's sections. Different areas of the vessel illuminate as scroll progresses. Each illuminated section corresponds to a supply category: deck supplies, engine room provisions, galley and catering, safety equipment, technical spares. The label for each category appears alongside its illuminated section.

The effect is that the ship itself is the navigation menu for this section. The company's categories are literally housed inside the vessel they serve.

---

## Section 7 — Vessel Types Served (Rising to Top View)

The camera begins to pull back. The blueprint dissolves and the real ship reappears. The camera continues to rise — above the ship, higher, until the view is directly overhead: a top-down sea view.

The ship below is now small. And from the four edges of the viewport — top, bottom, left, right — three additional vessels sail into frame. Four ships now occupy the scene: a container ship, a tanker, a bulk carrier, a cruise or passenger vessel. Each one is a different class, clearly distinct in silhouette from above.

The camera is stationary. The four ships settle into position, gently animated on the water.

**The numbers appear:** Above each vessel, a text block descends into position. It carries the vessel type name and, below it, a counter that animates upward from zero to the company's actual figure — vessels served, contracts completed, whatever is most meaningful. The counters stagger their start times, one vessel's numbers appearing slightly after the previous, so the reveals cascade across the screen rather than arriving simultaneously.

---

## Section 8 — Why Choose Us (To the Sky)

The camera pulls further back — past the clouds, the horizon recedes, the ocean becomes a gradient below. The sky fills the viewport. It is a clear, expansive sky: deep blue transitioning to lighter blue, a few clouds drifting at the horizon line, a quality of openness and altitude.

This section's content is delivered through a **carousel**. Each slide in the carousel carries a single reason — a brief headline and two lines of supporting copy. The carousel transitions with a fluid horizontal slide, or alternatively a vertical wipe, maintaining the sense of upward movement established by the camera's journey.

The carousel controls are minimal — arrows, or a dot indicator — and the entire carousel can optionally auto-advance at a comfortable pace. The sky background continues its subtle parallax drift behind the carousel, keeping the section alive.

---

## Section 9 — Global Network (Into Space / The Earth)

The sky darkens. Blue becomes indigo becomes black. Stars emerge. The atmosphere curves below, and rising from the bottom of the frame — or rotating into view from an angle — the Earth appears. A photorealistic, slowly rotating globe, its ocean surfaces textured, continents clear, cloud layer slowly drifting as a separate render layer above the surface.

The layout splits:

**Left half:** The Earth, gently rotating on its axis. It is the visual centrepiece — not a static infographic but a living planet.

**Right half:** A vertical panel. At the top, a fixed label — *"Our Network"* or *"Countries We Serve."* Below it, a country name displayed in clean, prominent typography. Every one or two seconds, the country name transitions to the next — the outgoing name slides upward and fades, the incoming name rises from below and resolves to full opacity. A small indicator — a flag, a continent label, or simply a count number — accompanies each name.

Simultaneously, as each country name appears on the right, a subtle highlight or pulse appears on that country's location on the globe on the left. The globe is not merely decorative — it responds to the text. The two halves of the screen are in conversation with each other.

This section can scroll freely — the globe stays fixed as the user scrolls, or the list of countries can be a long vertical scroll that drives the globe's highlight independently.

---

## Section 10 — Contact & Footer

The Earth remains on the left, rotating. The right panel transitions: the country counter fades away and a **contact form** slides in — fields for name, company, vessel type, message, and a submit action. The form is minimal, well-spaced, elegant. The Earth on the left continues rotating — the company's global presence watching over the enquiry being written.

Below the contact form, or as a full-width element beneath this split layout, the **footer** appears: company links, certifications, social handles, registered address — all in compact, refined typography.

The Earth fades very slowly as the user reaches the bottom of the page, until only the stars remain — a faint field of light behind the footer text.

## Technical Narrative Summary

| Section | Background State | Primary Technique |
|---|---|---|
| Preloader | Ship cabin interior, porthole closed | CSS/GSAP porthole open animation |
| Hero | Porthole open, live ocean scene | Three.js / Shader animated scene |
| Scroll Transition | Camera moves through porthole, descends | GSAP ScrollTrigger + R3F camera path |
| About | Underwater depth | R3F scene + scroll-opacity text reveal |
| Facts | Sea surface, ship present | R3F + GSAP card entrance + 3D flip |
| Supply Categories | Ship → Blueprint interior | GSAP morph / SVG blueprint transition |
| Vessel Types | Top-down sea view, four ships | R3F + GSAP counter + stagger |
| Why Choose Us | Open sky | GSAP carousel, R3F sky environment |
| Network | Space + revolving Earth | R3F globe + GSAP country switcher |
| Contact + Footer | Space / stars | Framer Motion form, Earth fade |

## Tone & Feel

The experience should feel like a documentary film about the sea — serious, confident, visually rich, and unexpectedly emotional for an industrial sector. The company does not feel like a vendor. It feels like a institution that has been part of the sea for decades. Every transition earns its drama. Nothing is decorative for its own sake — every visual choice serves the story of a company whose world is the ocean.
